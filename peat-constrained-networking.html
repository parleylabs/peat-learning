When the network won't cooperate

PEAT normally rides on QUIC, a modern internet protocol that quietly assumes three luxuries: every device has a network address, bandwidth runs into the millions of bits per second, and a reply comes back within a few thousandths of a second.

A great deal of the work PEAT is built for, though, happens where none of those luxuries are on offer — a ridgeline with no cell signal, a vessel far past the last tower, a disaster zone where the infrastructure is rubble. This track follows the mesh as its link thins out rung by rung, from roughly hundred-megabit Wi-Fi down toward a single satellite burst of fewer than two thousand bytes that a device might only get to send once an hour.

One thing to fix in your head before you start. Most of this track is a design for constrained operation, not a tour of shipped features. The two long-range links it leans on — satellite (Iridium SBD) and LoRa — do not exist in code yet; they are Proposed ADRs (ADR-051, ADR-052) with no crate behind them. What ships today is the part that runs over QUIC, Bluetooth Low Energy, and the peat-lite UDP bridge. The labels above appear on every claim so you always know which is which; where a number comes from a radio's datasheet rather than a PEAT measurement, the source line says so.
The design goal: as bandwidth collapses, PEAT keeps working by stepping down the ladder — swapping the transport beneath an unchanged mesh, rather than failing. Today the top two rungs are real (Wi-Fi/QUIC and Bluetooth/peat-lite); the LoRa and Iridium rungs are Proposed transports (ADR-052, ADR-051) with no code yet, and the rates shown on them are radio datasheet figures, not PEAT measurements.
fading bars = signal weakening highlighted rung = transport in use
This track assumes you've met the basics in the hub — the modules on peat-mesh, peat-btle/lite, and the gateway. From here on, we go where the internet stops. Keep one distinction handy throughout: a relay/hub node is an ordinary peat-mesh node that happens to sit on a busy link and merge traffic; the peat-gateway product is a separate control plane that observes and manages but never sits in the peer-to-peer data path. This track is about relay nodes.

01 · QUIC is not your address

The whole problem looks enormous until you separate two ideas that most networking quietly merges together: who a device is, and how you happen to reach it today.

In PEAT a device's stable identity comes from a key only that device holds — an Ed25519 signing key. The richer tiers of the stack name a device by its DeviceId: the SHA-256 hash of that public key, kept to its first 16 bytes (128 bits, 32 hex characters). Because it derives from a private key, the name is effectively impossible to forge, and it stays the same no matter how the device connects. QUIC only looks like it owns "addressing" because the library PEAT uses for it, Iroh, builds its own identity from that very same kind of key, so on an ordinary internet connection several layers stack neatly onto one piece of software. Shipped

One honest wrinkle worth knowing up front: there is no single 256-bit name used everywhere. The constrained tiers economize. A peat-lite or peat-btle node is named by a 4-byte (32-bit) NodeId — small enough to fit a tiny header, derived from the key or a radio MAC — and the transport layer carries its own string-form address as well. So "identity" is really a small family of names that bridge into one another at the boundaries, not one number stamped on every packet. Shipped

Source: peat-mesh/src/security/device_id.rs:39-47 (16-byte DeviceId); peat-lite/src/node_id.rs, peat-btle/src/security/identity.rs:141 (u32 NodeId). The earlier draft of this track said "256-bit NodeId" — no such value exists in code.

Pull those layers apart and a simple rule appears: the name never changes, while the delivery address is whatever a given radio happens to use today.

Move house, keep your name. Each transport maps the stable identity to its own kind of address — for the shipped radios that means an iroh endpoint string (QUIC) or a short BLE id; for the proposed radios it would mean a satellite modem's IMEI or a LoRa endpoint. The QUIC and BLE rows are Shipped; the satellite row is Proposed.
name — the stable identity (never changes) address — per transport (changes)
What this buys you: moving PEAT onto a new, non-internet radio never means reinventing identity. The transport interface that makes this possible — the MeshTransport trait seam — already ships Shipped, and ADR-032 describes the pattern (the ADR itself is Proposed). Bringing up a new radio is real work, not a one-liner, though: the LoRa and Satellite values already exist in the transport enum yet resolve to "no transport registered" precisely because the implementation behind them hasn't been written.
Source: peat-mesh/src/transport/mod.rs:375 (trait seam, shipped); ADR-032 (Proposed); peat-mesh/src/transport/manager.rs:1317 (LoRa/Satellite → None).

02 · The three layers

QUIC does three separate jobs at once, and on a normal network you never notice the seams. Off-grid, those jobs come apart, and each one needs a different replacement. This stack is the map for everything that follows.

AddressingTransportSync

The first two layers are mostly plumbing. They take real engineering, but the shape of the work is well understood, and PEAT already has the seams for it — a transport interface and a separate code crate for each radio. The third layer is where the genuine design problem lives, because making independent copies of data agree is exactly the thing that QUIC's behaviour was quietly helping with. 03 · Why CRDTs change everything

QUIC spends a lot of effort guaranteeing that every byte arrives exactly once, in the right order, with nothing lost. The data PEAT carries mostly doesn't need that guarantee, and understanding why is the key that opens the whole constrained world.

PEAT stores its shared state in CRDTs — Conflict-free Replicated Data Types. The mouthful matters less than one plain property: a CRDT update can be applied in any order, and applying the same update twice does nothing extra. Picture two people each keeping a copy of a shopping list and mailing postcards back and forth — "add milk," "cross off eggs." It makes no difference if a postcard shows up late, shows up twice, or arrives before one written earlier. Fold them all in, and both lists end up identical.

Late, duplicated, out of order — it all converges to the same state. The reliable, in-order delivery QUIC works so hard for turns out to be machinery this data never required.
✉️ = one CRDT update (a delta) a device holding its own copy

Compare that to reading a recipe aloud over a crackly phone line, where a single dropped or swapped word ruins the dish — that is the kind of protocol that genuinely breaks on a twenty-second satellite hop. Because PEAT behaves like the postcards instead, it can shed QUIC's delivery guarantees and lean on the mathematics of the data itself, which holds up even when the link loses, repeats, and delays messages.

There is a practical bonus on the thinnest links. For last-writer-wins values you can send the current value of something rather than its whole history. If a sensor reading changed fifty times while a device was out of contact, you transmit one number and one timestamp, not fifty updates — and peat-lite's data types are designed as small, bounded values precisely so this collapse is cheap. (Counters behave a little differently — they merge by taking the maximum per source — but they are bounded too.) Shipped

Source: Automerge sync (peat-mesh, default automerge-backend) and the peat-lite CRDTs that actually ship — LwwRegister and GCounter (peat-lite/src/{lww,counter}.rs). PnCounter is firmware-only and OrSet is a reserved wire byte with no implementation.

04 · Transports as a PACE ladder

Every radio strikes a different bargain between reach, speed, power, delay, and cost. The design intent is that PEAT registers all the ones a device carries and falls back among them with a planning method borrowed from the military: Primary, Alternate, Contingency, Emergency.

Status, plainly. The PACE ladder in this section is a design for how fallback should work, not a shipped state machine. Transport selection today is a simpler preference_order list in the TransportManager; multi-transport PACE failover is roadmap Phase 2 In flight/Proposed. And of the four rungs below, only the top two ride a transport that exists in code: Wi-Fi/QUIC and Bluetooth Low Energy are Shipped; LoRa and Iridium are Proposed (ADR-052, ADR-051) — no crate, no module. A min_pace_level floor is a teaching idea Speculative, not a field in the code.

The pattern across radios is consistent — the farther a signal needs to travel, the less data it can carry. Bluetooth Low Energy reaches a few hundred metres at roughly a couple of megabits per second on the radio (PHY) level. LoRa stretches from seven to nearly ninety kilometres, paid for by dropping to mere kilobytes per second. Iridium's satellite service reaches anywhere on Earth, pole to pole, but a single Short Burst Data message is capped near 1,960 bytes, takes five to twenty seconds to land, and costs roughly four to thirteen cents to send. The numbers below come from each radio's specifications — not from PEAT measurements — and for LoRa and Iridium there is no PEAT transport behind them yet.

05 · Dialects + the interpreter

The same software cannot run everywhere. PEAT's full sync engine, Automerge, expects a real operating system and a working-set measured in megabytes. A typical sensor microcontroller has about 256 kilobytes of RAM and no operating system at all. The gap is large enough — roughly two orders of magnitude — that the edge needs a leaner dialect.

A note on the numbers: peat-lite's 256 KB is a stated design target (ADR-035), not a budget any code enforces — there is no allocator cap or static-RAM assertion. The "Automerge needs ~10 MB" figure that earlier drafts cited does not appear as a measurement anywhere in the source, so the often-quoted "~40× smaller" ratio rests on two soft inputs and is best read as "much smaller," not a measured multiple.

The answer is two dialects of the same family of conflict-free data types, with a translator in the middle. Tiny devices speak peat-lite, whose messages ride a compact 16-byte fixed header. A better-resourced device — a phone, a Raspberry Pi, a small Linux box running peat-mesh with the lite-bridge feature — sits at the boundary and is fluent in both, translating the lightweight edge dialect into full Automerge for the richer mesh and back again. Shipped (the lite↔Automerge bridge; the cross-transport Translator codec ships under ADR-059, which is itself Proposed).

This bilingual device is a relay node, not the peat-gateway. The translation and merging happen in a peat-mesh node (the LiteMeshTransport bridge). The peat-gateway product is a separate control plane that implements no mesh transports and never sits in the sync path — it observes and manages. When this track draws a 🗼, read it as "a capable bridge node," not "the gateway product."
No constrained radio ever has to make the full engine survive a slow link. The edge sends a small update; the bridge node does the heavy merging on its behalf. (The BLE leg ships today; LoRa and satellite legs await those proposed transports.)

06 · Anti-entropy: one message an hour

Left alone, two copies of shared data drift apart as each picks up changes the other hasn't seen. "Anti-entropy" is the periodic ritual that pulls them back together: the two copies compare notes and trade whatever is missing. On a satellite link, where one exchange costs cents, takes up to twenty seconds, and may be the only one you get that hour, every byte of that comparison has to earn its place.

This whole section is a design, not a feature. The single-burst, digest-over-satellite reconciliation described here is Speculative — it exists nowhere in code, and it depends on the satellite transport that is itself Proposed. What PEAT actually ships for reconciliation is negentropy set reconciliation over Iroh QUIC Shipped — an interactive, multi-round-trip protocol that assumes a real QUIC channel, the opposite of a once-an-hour one-shot frame. The designs below are what a satellite or LoRa link would need; treat them as a sketch of unbuilt work, not a description of how PEAT behaves today.
Shipped reconciliation: peat-mesh/src/storage/negentropy_sync.rs + automerge_sync.rs (negentropy 0.5, ADR-040, issue #435), re-exported as NegentropySync in peat-protocol. Version-vector, snapshot-since-T, hierarchical-digest, and IBLT schemes are not implemented anywhere; peat-lite has no sync engine at all.

If a satellite transport did exist, Iridium's hardware would offer a useful property. By design, a single Iridium Short Burst Data session can both send a mobile-originated message and deliver a mobile-terminated one waiting at the Iridium ground station in the same exchange (the +SBDIX command) — a complete, if slow, round trip for the price of one. A reconciliation protocol built for that link would put a full exchange in one session: the outgoing burst carries recent changes plus a compact summary of what the device holds, and the reply carries back exactly what was missing.

The MO+MT-in-one-pass behaviour is an Iridium hardware fact (the +SBDIX AT command), not a PEAT capability — cited from Iridium SBD developer documentation / gbrain research/quic-iridium-sbd-feasibility. PEAT has no SBD code.
The summary is the clever part of the design: a device would describe what it has compactly enough that the relay node can work out what to send back, all within a single ~1,960-byte budget. Speculative — no such digest format is built.
up: your changes + summary down: what you were missing

Four candidate digest designs — every one of these is Speculative (teaching-only); none is implemented, and the byte budgets are illustrative arithmetic, not wire formats.

If the device remembers Speculative

It could keep a small "reading log" — the latest update number it has seen from each peer, on the order of 8 bytes apiece. A cell of up to a hundred devices would then fit in one satellite message with room left for real data. (Illustrative math — PEAT does not keep per-peer version vectors today.)

If it forgets on reboot Speculative

Many tiny devices keep nothing across a restart, so a device could instead send its own latest number and the time it last made contact, and the relay would reply with everything that changed since then, newest values first. (A "snapshot-since-T" scheme — also unbuilt.)

At larger scale Speculative

A summary for a thousand devices would be too big, so a device could carry a full log only for its own small group and a single fingerprint for each tier above it, reusing the roll-up summaries the hierarchy produces. (A hierarchical digest — design only.)

On faster radios Speculative

A LoRa link could afford a couple of exchanges and use a compact "difference sketch" (IBLT-style) sized to the expected gap, letting both sides find what differs without listing everything they share. (Note: LoRa itself is a proposed transport; the shipped reconciliation, negentropy, runs over QUIC, not LoRa.)

The hard part is deletion. If you only send current values, "this was deleted" looks the same as "I never heard of it." The fix is a small marker (a tombstone) that records a deletion, kept around at least as long as the slowest device might stay out of contact, with a relay node as the keeper of record. Tombstones themselves do ship — peat-node has tombstone GC with a 168-hour (7-day) default retention Shipped — but per-collection deletion-policy enforcement through the mesh, and the related sync-reliability work, is In flight.
Source: peat-node tombstone GC knobs (--tombstone-ttl-hours, 168h default, 300s GC interval) — issue peat-node#136; sync-reliability bugs #850/#829/#873. (An earlier draft cited "#857" here; #857 is actually the ADR-046 Phase-4 selectors epic, unrelated to deletion GC.)

07 · Router, not broker

A design where many sensors all sync through one busy relay sits dangerously close to the kind of central broker PEAT was built to avoid. The distinction that keeps it honest is the difference between a place where roads meet and a seat of government.

The relay node is a meeting point, not a ruler. It holds the most complete copy of the data simply because that is where the long-range traffic happens to land — the same way a mountain town ends up busy because it sits on the only pass. As long as the relay only merges data (a deterministic step that any device with the same updates would arrive at identically) and passes it along, it holds no special authority. The danger line is crossed only if it starts deciding disputes by its own judgment instead of by the shared merge rule. (This node is a peat-mesh relay, not the peat-gateway control plane, which never sits in the data path at all.) Shipped — Automerge merge is deterministic.

The test is simple: remove the busy relay and watch. A broker was the network, so the network dies with it. In PEAT the data already lives, copied, across the devices — the cell deterministically re-elects a leader (no votes, no quorum) and a capable node takes over the relay job. The local re-election and reconnect are Shipped; automatic failover across transports (e.g. onto a satellite uplink) is not a demonstrated path.
✖ = removed / failed relay device gone dark new relay node taking over
Source: deterministic leader election (peat-mesh/src/hierarchy/dynamic_strategy.rs:190-224, shipped) + peat-node auto-reconnect watchdog (5 s→120 s backoff). Cross-transport uplink failover is undemonstrated.

08 · Two graphs, one system

It helps to see that any deployment really has two different maps drawn over the same devices. One map shows which devices can physically reach each other. The other shows which devices actually exchange state with each other. They are not the same map, and the gap between them is a deliberate choice driven by what each link costs.

Use the buttons to switch between the two maps for one small system — three sensors near each other, a relay node with a satellite dish, and a command post on the far side of the satellite. (The local links are Shipped transports; the satellite hop is the Proposed one, drawn dashed.)

What can reach what: the three sensors are in radio range of each other and of the relay node, and the relay can reach the command post only by way of the satellite.
local radio link — cheap satellite hop — slow & paid full two-way sync summary only
The point you'd miss otherwise: you could have all five devices sync with each other as one flat mesh across the satellite — nothing stops you. You wouldn't, though, because every peer-to-peer exchange would then cost two satellite hops — on the order of forty seconds and two paid messages, using the Iridium ~20 s / per-message figures from section 04. So the dense, everyone-with-everyone syncing stays on the cheap local links, and the satellite carries only a compact summary up and commands down. "Decentralized" describes who holds authority over the data, not a promise that every device chats with every other. Speculative — the summary-up/commands-down satellite topology assumes the proposed SBD transport and the unbuilt digest of section 06; the local-mesh half is real.

09 · One node, three dials

A mute telemetry sensor and a fully coordinating peer run the same software — they differ only in how three independent dials are set. The three "dials" are a teaching abstraction, not three fields in the code — but each maps onto something real: reachability to the registered transports, participation to which collections a node syncs, and authority to its RBAC Role (Shipped: Leader / Member / Observer / Commander / Admin).

Sensor A — mute sensor. It sits inside the local cell on short-range radio (BLE or similar) with no uplink of its own. It publishes its own readings and never coordinates with its peers, at the lowest authority; any command for it arrives down through the relay node. The wider mesh effectively begins there.
local sync — cheap satellite path — paid amber ring = selected device
Notice that the everyday, high-volume coordination all happens on the cheap local links among A, B and their neighbours, while the (proposed) satellite would carry only the summary up and commands back down — exactly the choice section 08 was pointing at. Changing a device's job is a matter of turning these three dials, never swapping its software. Shipped for the local-link behaviour and the role/collection model; Proposed for the satellite uplink.

10 · Match coordination to cost

The single most useful design rule for these systems follows directly from the last two sections: put the boundaries of your hierarchy wherever a link gets expensive.

Coordination that is almost free over a local Bluetooth link becomes punishing over satellite. Each back-and-forth between two peers turns into two satellite hops — on the order of forty seconds of delay and two paid messages, using the Iridium figures from section 04 — so a chatty exchange that would finish instantly nearby could take many minutes and real money across the sky. The way out is to let devices that are physically close form a tight local group and coordinate freely there, and to let the expensive long-range link carry only the distilled result upward and commands downward. Speculative as stated — the satellite math assumes the proposed SBD transport; the principle (keep dense traffic on cheap links) applies to any real long-range link too.

Keep the dense traffic on the cheap side of the cliff; send only the aggregate across it. The satellite link should sit between groups, never inside one.
cheap local links summary across the cliff cost cliff (tier boundary)
The rule of thumb: place each tier boundary at a jump in link cost. That one decision often decides whether the satellite bill is survivable. (PEAT's hierarchy sizing — a cell of ~4–13 nodes, a cohort of a few cells — is design-intent guidance in the code's comments, not a limit any code enforces, so this placement is a deployment judgement, not an automatic one.)

11 · Configuring both

Most real deployments need both kinds of coordination at once: a shared picture that everyone agrees on, and direct orders aimed at specific devices. The good news is that this choice lives on each collection of data, not on the device, so a single system runs both side by side.

You define each collection with the profile its data deserves. Positions and sensor readings want a "shared picture" profile — broadcast to the whole group, merged by everyone. Orders want a "directed tasking" profile — sent only to chosen recipients, writable only by an authorized commander, and acknowledged on receipt.

The YAML below is illustrative, not a copy of a shipped schema. The shared-picture profile is close to what ships: peat-node collection config (ADR-016, peat-node#55) persists per-collection TTL and deletion policy, QoS priority is real, and lww maps to a shipped CRDT. The directed-tasking profile is mostly Proposed/Speculative: targeted delivery is the ADR-046 epic In flight (#853), and command_log is a CRDT that does not exist anywhere yet.

Real wire CRDT codes: lww=0x01, gcounter=0x02, pncounter=0x03, orset=0x04 (peat-lite/src/protocol/crdt_type.rs) — but orset is a reserved byte with no implementation, and TTLs are defined per CRDT type in seconds, not as a collection-level 5m field. write_role must be a real RBAC role (Leader/Member/Observer/Commander/Admin) — there is no operator role. min_pace and a string ttl are not fields in the code.

The mute sensor from section 09 needs no third profile. It simply uses each of these in one direction only: it publishes into a shared-picture collection it never reads back, and it listens to a tasking collection it never writes to.

One real gap remains, and it matters for autonomy under human authority. peat-lite's current data types are safe for facts and counts, but none is safe for orders. Today a command is an ordinary JSON document in a commands collection; settling two conflicting commands by "newest wins" could quietly fire the wrong one. A tasking collection needs a command_log type that records orders in sequence, marks superseded ones plainly, and is write-gated to an authorized commander — so a person can always see the chain and a machine never acts outside it. The shared-picture half works today Shipped; the targeted-delivery half is the ADR-046 work already underway In flight (#853); the command-log type itself still has to be designed Speculative.

12 · Gaps & where to go next

A quick honest map of what is solid, what is being built, and what still needs design — plus the doors out of this track.

built — works today in flight — being built proposed / to design — open

Reminder: the broadcast-sync backbone (Automerge + negentropy over QUIC) and the QUIC/BLE/peat-lite transports are real and tested. The long-range transports this track leans on — SBD and LoRa — are Proposed ADRs with no code. The honest contribution map below is where a new engineer can move the needle.

Open questions worth your attention

  • The SBD and LoRa transport crates Proposed — the whole premise of this track. Implement the shipped MeshTransport trait for each radio (ADR-051, ADR-052). Largest greenfield, highest leverage.
  • The command-log data type Speculative — recording orders in sequence and marking superseded ones, write-gated and acknowledged, safe for real-world action under human authority. Does not exist yet.
  • The single-burst digest format Speculative — how a "reading log" and an "everything since this time" form share one compact message over a non-interactive link (negentropy assumes interactive QUIC).
  • Deletion-policy enforcement In flight — tombstones ship with a 7-day default, but per-collection deletion-policy enforcement through the mesh, and the related sync-reliability bugs, are open (peat-node#136; #850/#829/#873).
  • Encryption for the proposed radios Proposed — the residual FIPS conflict is confined to ADR-052 (LoRa), which specifies ChaCha20-Poly1305 and is unbuilt. The shipped constrained transports are already FIPS-clean: peat-btle uses AES-256-GCM + ECDH-P256, and peat-lite has no symmetric crypto of its own. Fixing ADR-052's cipher to AES-256-GCM, and designing an object-security (OSCORE/AES-CCM-style) per-message wrapper so data stays protected through an untrusted relay, are the open items.
FIPS posture (ground truth §6): shipped crypto migrated off ChaCha20/X25519 to AES-256-GCM + ECDH-P256 on 2026-05-18 (peat-mesh rc.12, ADR-060 §5). Stale READMEs still mention ChaCha20 — a doc bug, not shipped behaviour. Algorithms are FIPS-approved; the pure-Rust modules are not CMVP-validated (peat-btle#75, in flight).
